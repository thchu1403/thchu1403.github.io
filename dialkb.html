Dial Keyboard — Input Experience Research

As the boundaries between creative work and hybrid collaboration blur, keyboard input has become more than just typing. This multi-phase research project investigated how a customizable dial control, dedicated meeting shortcuts, and seamless multi-device Bluetooth connectivity could transform the keyboard experience for creative professionals and knowledge workers. Across three studies and nearly 2,000 participants, the research uncovered what users truly need from a next-generation input device.

I was the lead UX researcher across all three research phases — from initial dial concept validation to shortcut mapping for collaboration tools and multi-device connectivity studies. I designed every survey instrument, recruited and screened participants, and translated raw data into prioritized design direction for the product team.

Approach

Study 1. Dial Concept & Creative Professional Needs

The first phase focused on validating the concept of a customizable rotary dial integrated into a keyboard for creative professionals — a direct response to workflows in Adobe Suite, Blender, Maya, and CAD tools, where repetitive parameter adjustments (brush size, timeline scrubbing, zoom) slow creative flow. I designed screener surveys to identify qualified participants by software usage and creative hours, then ran a large-scale US survey with 574 participants. Key findings: ~60–70% of creatives saw immediate value in dial-based controls, valuing faster access to frequently-used actions. Top concerns centered on learning curve and the complexity of customization setup.


Study 2. Keyboard Shortcuts for Collaboration Tools

As hybrid work grew, a second research phase investigated a different pain point: friction during virtual meetings. I conducted a dedicated shortcut priority study with 584 participants to understand which meeting controls (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet, WebEx) users found most frustrating and which they most wanted dedicated keyboard keys for. Mute/Unmute and Camera on/off ranked as the top two priorities by a significant margin — followed by screen sharing, chat, and participant panel. I also ran visual concept testing on icon designs for these shortcut keys, validating which visual language best communicated each function at a glance.


Keyboard Switch Type Comparative Testing

Alongside the shortcut study, I ran a comparative evaluation of three mechanical keyboard switch types — Blue (Clicky/tactile feedback), Brown (Tactile but quieter), and Red (Linear/smooth) — to understand user preferences across different use contexts: creative work, quiet office environments, and extended typing sessions. The testing evaluated both subjective feel and objective acoustic preference, providing the hardware team with clear evidence for which switch profile best fits the target user base.



Study 3. Bluetooth Multi-Device Connectivity

The third phase addressed one of the most common friction points for knowledge workers: switching a keyboard between multiple devices. With 836 participants, I investigated how users expect multi-device Bluetooth pairing to work, which connection method they prefer (USB dongle vs. Bluetooth), and how physical button layouts and icons best communicate channel switching. I tested multiple icon concepts — Bluetooth symbols, device number labels, and device-type icons — identifying the most intuitive design language for a 3-channel keyboard. ~50% of participants were comfortable self-setting up a new peripheral; 35% needed some guidance, informing onboarding recommendations.


Synthesis & Product Recommendations

Across all three studies, I synthesized findings into a unified set of design recommendations covering: the dial's core use cases and customization model, the priority shortcut key set for hybrid collaboration, switch type selection for the target audience, Bluetooth button design language and channel-switching interaction, and onboarding guidance for new users. Each recommendation was backed by quantitative data and delivered to the product team with clear prioritization to guide hardware and software development decisions.
