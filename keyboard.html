Keyboard Input Experience Research

What does a keyboard need to be in an era of creative work, hybrid collaboration, and multi-device computing? This multi-phase research program explored three distinct frontiers of keyboard input: a customizable rotary dial for creative professionals, dedicated shortcut keys for virtual collaboration, and seamless multi-device Bluetooth connectivity for knowledge workers. Across three independent studies and nearly 2,000 participants, the program built an evidence base that directly shaped product strategy — from hardware feature prioritization to icon design and out-of-box experience.

I was the lead UX researcher across all three studies, responsible for study design, screener development, participant recruitment, and synthesis. Each study addressed a distinct user need; together they formed a connected body of knowledge about where keyboard input is heading and what users expect from it.

Approach

Step 1. Screener Design & Participant Recruitment

To ensure participants genuinely represented the target audience, I designed a detailed screener survey before the main study. Qualification criteria focused on creative software usage (Adobe Creative Suite, Blender, Maya, CAD tools) and weekly creative hours. This segmentation was critical — it ensured that feedback on the dial concept came from users whose workflows would actually benefit from rotary input, rather than general keyboard users.

Step 2. Large-Scale Concept Validation Survey

With 574 qualified US participants, the survey validated the core dial concept and mapped the most compelling use cases. ~60–70% of creatives saw immediate value in dial-based controls, with the highest appeal in tasks involving repetitive parameter adjustments: brush size, zoom level, timeline scrubbing, and layer opacity. Top concerns centered on the learning curve for customization and setup complexity — insights that directly shaped the product's onboarding experience and default mapping strategy.

Use Case: Creative Tool Shortcuts

Approach

Step 1. Collaboration App Pain Points & Shortcut Demand

With 379 external keyboard users qualified through screener criteria — daily keyboard usage, collaboration app reliance, and function key adoption — I mapped the friction points driving demand for dedicated hardware shortcuts. Participants reported consistent pain across Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet, and WebEx: inconsistent control placement between platforms, mis-clicks on crowded UIs, and the inability to trigger meeting actions without first switching focus away from the active window. Screen sharing, mute, and camera controls emerged as the highest-friction moments. Over 70% of participants said dedicated keyboard shortcuts would meaningfully improve their meeting productivity.

Step 2. Dedicated Shortcut Priority Ranking

I asked participants to rank which collaboration actions they most wanted as dedicated hardware keys — surfacing a clear "core trio": Mute/Unmute ranked #1, Camera On/Off ranked #2, and Share Screen ranked #3, each by a significant margin over other controls. Beyond the core trio, participants also wanted quick access to chat panel, participant list, reactions, and captions. The data gave the product team a prioritized shortcut key set backed by both frequency of use and perceived time-criticality — the two factors that most strongly predicted demand for a physical key.

Step 3. Programmable Keys Usage & Interest

A separate section of the study examined programmable key behavior: 113 current users (30% of the sample) shared how they already assigned custom functions, while the remaining 257 users expressed their intent if such keys were available. Top applications for key assignment were Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and — notably — ChatGPT, which ranked above traditional tools like Excel and Outlook, signaling AI tool access as an emerging hardware priority. More than 75% of all participants rated programmable keys as highly beneficial. The main adoption barrier was not concept rejection but setup complexity: users wanted the capability, but needed a simpler configuration experience to actually use it.

Approach

Step 1. Connection Method & Pairing Preference

The third study addressed one of the most common pain points for knowledge workers who move between a laptop, desktop, and personal device: switching a keyboard between multiple machines. With 836 participants, I investigated preferences between USB dongle and direct Bluetooth connectivity, how users expect multi-device switching to work physically, and what interaction model (button placement, press behavior) felt most intuitive for channel switching on a 3-channel keyboard.

Step 2. Channel Icon Concept Testing

I tested multiple icon design concepts for the three Bluetooth channel keys — comparing Bluetooth symbols with numeric labels, device-type icons (laptop, phone, tablet), and hybrid approaches. Testing evaluated immediate comprehension, learnability, and confidence in switching. Findings identified the most intuitive design language for channel indication, providing the industrial design and software teams with a validated visual direction for the keyboard's physical key markings and the companion software UI.

Step 3. Onboarding & Setup Confidence

A dedicated section of the study measured user confidence around peripheral setup. ~50% of participants felt comfortable setting up a new multi-device keyboard entirely on their own, while ~35% reported needing some guidance — and ~15% strongly preferred a guided setup flow. These findings directly informed the design of the out-of-box experience, pairing wizard, and help documentation, ensuring the product reached both confident power users and first-time Bluetooth keyboard owners.

Spanning three research studies, this project built a holistic understanding of what creative professionals and knowledge workers need from a next-generation keyboard — from customizable dial controls and collaboration shortcuts to seamless multi-device connectivity. The findings directly shaped the dial keyboard product strategy — driving hardware switch selection, UI design language for channel keys, feature prioritization for the shortcut key set, and the design of the out-of-box pairing experience. The ChatGPT programmable key finding surfaced an AI hardware opportunity that influenced the broader keyboard roadmap.
